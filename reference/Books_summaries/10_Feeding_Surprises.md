# 10 Feeding Surprises

This supplement for Vampire: The Masquerade presents ten dramatic scenarios that can occur during the act of feeding, designed to add tension, drama, and unexpected complications to hunting scenes. The book provides ready-to-use surprises that can be inserted into any game, whether feeding is handled quickly or as a central dramatic element of the story.

## Purpose and Scope

- This book contains 10 feeding surprises that can heighten drama, lead to further stories, or provide moments of dark humor.
- The scenarios avoid "usual suspects" like Lupines, True Faith, wizards, or fae, focusing instead on more unexpected encounters.
- Designed to work whether hunting is an integral part of the story or handled more abstractly.
- Each surprise includes suggestions for how to use it and potential story directions.

## Introduction

The book opens with a quote from Jean Anthelme Brillat-Savarin: "Tell me what you eat and I will tell you what you are." Feeding is central to what makes a vampire a vampire - the act of drinking blood. In some games, hunting and feeding are glossed over, while in others they are central to the story. These surprises can be inserted into any game to add unexpected complications.

## Chapter One: 10 Feeding Surprises

### 1: Holy crap, it's you!

The target of the feeding is a minor or local celebrity that the vampire actually knows, and possibly is a fan of. It could be a member of an obscure goth band, a local news reporter, a niche author, a YouTube or Twitch personality, or similar. The two most important things are: the celebrity is very minor (cannot reach a national audience), and the character actually knows who the person is. This encounter can lead in many directions - the character may forego feeding, try to fanboy, critique their work, turn the encounter into a new contact, or even take their victim as a new ghoul.

### 2: Do you know who my father is?

Something the character does before, during, or after the feeding causes the target to call upon their parentage - or the character steals the victim's wallet and recognizes the name. The drama comes from the possibility that a powerful and/or influential parent will learn their child has been violated. It need not be someone's child - could be a brother, sister, or spouse of someone with significant wealth, power, and/or influence. The powerful parent could be a gang boss, high-ranking lawyer, wealthy business tycoon, media personality, local politician, or even the mayor. If discovered before feeding, the character may break it off. If afterwards, the character must prevent their victim from relating what happened. Crafty characters may turn this to their advantage, using it to access power and influence.

### 3: I think I love you.

This surprise works best if the character uses seduction as their hunting method, but considering the pleasure of the Kiss, it may happen to anyone who leaves their victim alive. The target gains an unhealthy fixation on the character and becomes a stalker. This is a massive threat to the Masquerade, especially if the character doesn't realize they're being stalked. The stalker may see and record things of a supernatural nature. A stalker is obsessive and mentally unhinged, willing to take drastic actions. They will be violently jealous of anyone the character shows affection towards. Should the character see their stalker as a resource, turning their former victim into a ghoul will result in a massive increase in obsessive tendencies.

### 4: You haven't changed a bit.

The character's intended victim is someone who knew them when they were alive - someone who recognizes them. This works best if the character has stayed local. Depending on the time frame, reactions can range from "You look the same" to "What's your secret? No gluten?" to "You haven't changed a bit in 30 years!" This has the potential to threaten the Masquerade. If the character has surviving family or friends they've cut ties with (especially by faking their own death), meeting someone who knows them can have very dangerous repercussions. Such a meeting can also stir up old emotions - imagine a bullied kid meeting their bully after being Embraced, or meeting an old crush, one-night stand, or former best friend.

### 5: You're on what?

The character's victim is on drugs, and now those drugs are affecting the character too. This is very appropriate for someone who feeds in a club. Before using this surprise, research the effects of various recreational drugs. Some give boundless energy, some make you lethargic, some cause hallucinations. Self-Control or Courage rolls might be needed. The effect stays with the character for as long as the drugged blood stays in their system, or until the next night. In addition to the trouble the character might cause while under the influence (up to and including breaching the Masquerade), there is also the possibility of addiction.

### 6: Typhoid Mary

A relative of the previous surprise, except the victim suffers from some blood-borne disease rather than being under the influence of drugs. This disease now lurks in the character's blood until the tainted vitae is used up. The character has a chance of infecting anyone they bite and will certainly transfer the disease if they feed their blood to someone else. This surprise is especially devastating for characters that have a herd or who maintain a large amount of ghouls, as the disease can run rampant through the character's servants or vessels. The old standby is AIDS, which has the added bonus of being incurable. However, know your group - if any players have had a certain disease or someone close to them has, you probably don't want to bring it up in the game.

### 7: That's poaching.

The character inadvertently feeds on a mortal or ghoul belonging to another vampire. This can be discovered during the feeding (when the character tastes the presence of vampire blood or the mortal seems to have too good an idea of what's going on), afterwards (as the victim relates their experience to their master), or before the feeding (though in this case there is less drama). How the affronted party reacts depends on who the Kindred affected is. Someone who is already antagonistic or who puts stock in domain and the sanctity of resource may raise it as a point to the city's Elders or even the prince. A neutral Kindred might be turned into an enemy, while an ally could simply say "You owe me one" and let it slide. One interesting variation is to arrange it so that one character feeds on a mortal that is part of another character's domain, letting the players play out the confrontation.

### 8: Hey, I'm a vampire too.

This surprise requires the right setting, like an underground club or possibly some kind of convention. The character's intended victim turns out to be a mortal who is into some kind of vampire subculture - it may be just a sexual thing, role-playing, or an individual who has embraced an "unlifestyle". The target mistakes the character for "one of his own" and may be in for the time of their life or a rude awakening. If you want to up the ante, perhaps the character's target is an actual vampire - they may be new to the city, or maybe the character is the newcomer, or the intended victim is a neonate recently released (or, even more interestingly, not released yet). If the city is large enough and the Kindred population big enough, it might even be that the two vampires just haven't met yet.

### 9: I know what you did.

This surprise works best if the character is doing a violent feeding, like an ambush or snatch-and-grab. Unbeknownst to them, their victim is being watched somehow. Maybe they have chosen an undercover cop or a criminal under police surveillance. Maybe their target is being followed by a detective because their spouse suspects them of cheating. Or maybe their victim has bodyguards who keep a respectful distance. Or it could be as simple as the target having one more friend who just stepped off for a bathroom break or a smoke. Whatever the case, the character is now faced with one or more witnesses, possibly with recorded evidence. At the very least, it looks like they have mugged someone. In the most extreme case, there is now a Masquerade-threatening cell phone video out there.

### 10: I… don't feel so… good.

This surprise requires the character to not want to kill their victim. However, they have chosen someone with a weak constitution, a heart defect, or some other condition that means the stress of the feeding (or possibly just the fear of realizing what's about to happen) kills them. In addition to the effect this might have on the character's humanity, there is also the question of what to do with the body. Most characters are unlikely to know how to properly dispose of a corpse, and depending on where the death happened, they might not have long to act. In addition, if people saw them with the victim before he died and the target then disappears, the character could easily be a person of interest in a police investigation, something that can easily threaten the Masquerade.
