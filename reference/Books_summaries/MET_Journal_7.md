# MET Journal 7

The document is the seventh issue of the Mind's Eye Theatre Journal, a quarterly publication focused on enhancing live-action roleplaying experiences within the World of Darkness universe.

## Introduction to Mind's Eye Theatre Journal

- The Journal is a quarterly publication dedicated to filling gaps in the White Wolf LARP environment.
- It provides rules for new character types, original storylines, guides for game management, and rules updates not found in other Mind's Eye releases.
- This issue delves into the acting side of live-action roleplaying, with Cynthia Summers and Matthew Hooper providing insight on making characters come to life.

## Staying in Character

Cynthia Summers provides comprehensive guidance on maintaining character during live-action roleplaying games.

### Challenges to Staying in Character

- **Out-of-Character Conversations**: People discussing movies, quoting Monty Python, or reuniting with friends can break immersion.
- **Physical Needs**: Eating, drinking, and sleeping can disrupt the mood, particularly for those playing non-living characters.
- **Boredom**: Sitting by yourself doing nothing is a great temptation to slide out of character.

### Solutions

- **Designated Out-of-Play Areas**: Storytellers should designate a spot as out-of-play and announce it at the beginning of the night.
- **Character-Appropriate Activities**: Bring things your character could do to keep busy - books, tools of your craft, games (chess, backgammon, go, mancala, mah jong, cards).
- **In-Character Excuses**: When leaving for other tasks, excuse yourself in character (making a phone call, checking email, answering a page, feeding the cat).
- **Character Refreshment**: If a character has gone stale, consider a brief walkabout or time away. Some downtime may bring you back refreshed.

### Benefits of Staying in Character

- Total immersion in the scene
- Enhanced roleplaying experience
- Better interaction with other players
- Maintaining game mood and atmosphere

## Deadguy Speaks: Dealing with Non-LARPers

Richard Dansky's column addresses the challenge of maintaining relationships with friends who don't play LARP.

### The Communication Problem

- Non-gamers see LARP as a "game" like chess or Parcheesi - you play, someone wins, and it's over.
- LARPers see it as a chronicle - a long-running series of interconnected stories carried out over years.
- This fundamental misunderstanding causes friction in relationships.

### The Commitment Spiral

- Players invest innumerable hours into plans, alliances, downtime reports, in-character emails, costuming, and maneuvering for power.
- Missing a session can mean losing ground to rivals, having plans come to naught, or missing crucial events.
- This creates an ever-widening spiral of commitment that looks like obsessive-compulsive behavior to outsiders.

### Finding Balance

- Recognize that friends can appreciate your hobby without wanting to participate.
- Don't treat every non-LARPer as "ripe fodder for recruitment."
- Find middle ground - appreciate that others have different interests.
- Maintain perspective on what's truly important.

The Journal serves as a bridge between official publications, providing:
- Material that couldn't fit in core rulebooks
- Practical guidance for players and Storytellers
- Community advice and social dynamics
- Rules for less common character types
