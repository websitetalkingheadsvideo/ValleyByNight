### 🎞️ *Opening Cutscene: “What the Walls Hear”*

**[INT. DUNLAP APARTMENTS – UNIT 3 – NIGHT]**

The room is dark, lit only by a single bare bulb over a small kitchen table. The light hums faintly. Shadows cling to the corners of the apartment, stretching longer than they should.

Mrs. Chen sits perfectly straight at the table. Hands folded. Eyes downcast. A cup of untouched tea steams gently in front of her.

Across from her, half in shadow, **Missfortune** stands near the window. Streetlight bleeds through warped glass, bending her silhouette into something slightly wrong — elongated, fractured, as if reflected through an unseen mirror.

**[VO – MRS. CHEN]:**
*“Sometimes… you deal with monsters.”*

Missfortune turns her head just enough that one eye catches the light. It does not blink.

Mrs. Chen continues calmly, as if discussing plumbing or rent.

> **Mrs. Chen:** “They were in the park again. After midnight. Music, but no source. Three of them this time.”

Missfortune smiles — not cruelly, not kindly. Just *knowingly*.

> **Missfortune:** “And?”

Mrs. Chen lifts her eyes. For a moment, her expression subtly shifts — softer, more open, almost *dreamlike*. Then it settles back into neutral.

> **Mrs. Chen:** “They did not notice me.”

Missfortune moves closer. The light flickers as she passes beneath it. For an instant, the room feels upside down — gravity uncertain, shadows clinging to the ceiling.

**[VO – MRS. CHEN]:**
*“Sometimes… they don’t know you’re watching.”*

Missfortune reaches out, gently straightening Mrs. Chen’s collar — an intimate, proprietary gesture.

> **Missfortune:** “Good. Monsters are easiest to study when they think you’re harmless.”

Mrs. Chen nods once.

Outside, faint laughter drifts through the walls — distant, melodic, wrong.

The tea cup trembles slightly. Mrs. Chen steadies it with one finger.

Silence.

**[CUT TO BLACK]**

**VALLEY BY NIGHT – THE QUIET CONSTANT**



[EXT. DUNLAP APARTMENTS – BEHIND THE FOUR-PLEX – NIGHT]

A narrow dirt path winds up a small rise behind the apartments. The city sounds fade with each step — traffic dulls, insects grow louder, time seems to loosen.

The camera follows Mrs. Chen from behind as she walks the path alone.

She passes a low, overgrown shrub. Its leaves shiver though there is no wind.

Beyond it, the world subtly changes.

The grass grows too green. The air hums faintly, like a distant chord held just a moment too long. Stones emerge from the earth in a loose ring — smooth, pale, arranged with quiet intention.

At the center: a fairy circle.

Light pools where it shouldn’t. Colors feel deeper, warmer. Shadows bend inward, respectful.

Mrs. Chen stops at the edge of the circle.

She does not step inside.

She watches.

Firefly-like motes drift upward, tracing slow, impossible spirals. Somewhere, unheard music plays — sweet, distant, and full of promises no one should make.

Mrs. Chen’s face softens again, just slightly. Not awe. Not fear.

Recognition.

[VO – MRS. CHEN]:
“…so you can see magic.”

For a brief moment, her reflection appears in the air above the stones — not distorted, not monstrous — simply different. Older. Quieter. Watching from the other side.

The lights dim. The music fades.

Mrs. Chen turns away, already forgetting details that should never be remembered clearly.

The fairy circle remains.

Waiting.
