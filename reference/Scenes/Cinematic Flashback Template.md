Below is a **clean, repeatable Flashback Format Template** designed specifically for *Valley by Night*. It’s tight, cinematic, and intentionally incomplete—meant to **introduce major plot threads** without explaining them.

---

# Valley by Night — Cinematic Flashback Template

## Purpose

Cinematic flashbacks are **not exposition**.
They are **memory fragments**—biased, incomplete, emotionally charged moments that signal importance and foreshadow consequences.

Use **sparingly**. One flashback should *reframe* a plot, not summarize it.

---

## Runtime

**30–60 seconds**
Ideal: **45 seconds**

---

## Structural Beats (Fixed Order)

### 1. Trigger Frame (2–4 seconds)

A single, static image that anchors the memory.

* No dialogue
* Minimal motion
* Ambient sound only

Examples:

* Cigarette ember flaring in darkness
* A hand tightening on a wine glass
* Desert wind moving curtains

This frame tells the audience *when* they are without saying it.

---

### 2. Establishing Moment (5–8 seconds)

The setting appears, already in motion.

* One location only
* No wide lore context
* Let the space speak

Sound:

* Distant traffic
* Muffled voices
* A hum, drip, or echo

Avoid narration.

---

### 3. Human Action (10–15 seconds)

Someone does something small—but loaded.

* A glance held too long
* A refusal to answer
* A name spoken quietly

Dialogue (if any):

* One or two lines max
* Never explanatory
* Should make sense *later*, not now

---

### 4. Disruption / Slip (8–12 seconds)

This is the **moment that matters**.

* A mask slips
* Someone reacts incorrectly
* An emotion shows that shouldn’t

Supernatural elements (if present):

* Subtle
* Unnamed
* Treated as *wrong*, not flashy

This is what causes fallout.

---

### 5. Cut Before Consequence (3–5 seconds)

End the scene **before** the result.

* A breath held
* A door about to open
* Someone realizing they were seen

No resolution.

---

### 6. End Card (Mandatory)

```
[CUT TO BLACK]

VALLEY BY NIGHT
```

Optional subtitle (rare):

```
— SOME THINGS ARE NOT FORGOTTEN —
```

Hold black for **2–3 seconds** minimum.

---

## Visual Language Rules (Consistency Matters)

Flashbacks should *always* feel distinct from present-day scenes.

**Recommended treatments:**

* Desaturated or warm-amber grading
* Light film grain or VHS noise
* Softer focus, shallower depth
* Fewer camera moves
* Hard cuts over dissolves

Once players recognize this look, the flashback becomes a narrative signal.

---

## Sound Design Rules

* Ambient sound > music
* Music, if used, should:

  * Fade in late
  * Never resolve
* Dialogue slightly muffled or distant
* Sound may drop out entirely in final seconds

Silence is allowed. Silence is powerful.

---

## Content Rules (Hard Constraints)

Do **not** include:

* Mechanics
* Clan explanations
* Titles or ranks
* Full names unless already known
* “As you know…” dialogue

Do include:

* Power imbalance
* Emotional subtext
* Incomplete information
* A reason the city *remembers* this moment

---

## When to Use a Flashback

Use one to introduce:

* A major NPC
* A faction shift
* The consequence of an unseen act
* A lie that will later unravel
* A truth that was noticed too early

Never use flashbacks for:

* Routine scenes
* Minor NPCs
* Rule explanations
* Player backstory (unless explicitly unlocked)

---

## Optional Advanced Rule: Mutable Flashbacks

The **same flashback may reappear later**, altered:

* Longer
* Clearer
* Revealing who misremembered—or lied

Same moment. Different truth.
