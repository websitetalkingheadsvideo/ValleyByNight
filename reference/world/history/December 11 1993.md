December 11, 1993 — The Mill Avenue Elysium & the Birth of a Coterie

Date: Saturday, December 11, 1993
Occasion: 2nd Saturday Elysium
Location: Hawthorn Estate, Phoenix

During the December 11, 1993 Elysium, Sarah Hansen formally claimed Mill Avenue in Tempe as her feeding ground. Rather than petitioning in advance, Sarah acted decisively and publicly, presenting a valid boon owed by Julien Roche, who had previously discussed making a similar claim but had not yet acted on it. Sarah invoked the boon, compelling Julien to back down without protest.

Prince Solomon Reaves ruled immediately and publicly. Impressed by Sarah’s initiative, her clean execution of Camarilla custom, and the absence of disruption, Solomon granted her Camarilla Status: Influential and formally recognized her claim to Mill Avenue. Julien was neither punished nor rewarded, marking the incident as a lesson rather than a reprimand.

Contrary to expectation, the event caused no lasting resentment. Julien was intrigued rather than angered by Sarah’s perspective—particularly her views on performance, audience, and transgressive art. Though their artistic philosophies differed sharply, Julien found her approach intellectually compelling. This mutual fascination, combined with overlapping interests in the Valley’s evolving art scene, led to continued association rather than rivalry.

Formation of the Art Coterie

Not long after the Mill Avenue Elysium, Sarah Hansen and Julien Roche formed a coterie with Harold Ashby. The coterie centered itself around:

Tracking emerging art trends in Phoenix, Tempe, and Scottsdale

Gallery openings, underground shows, and performance spaces

The intersection of mortal art culture and Kindred influence

Unknown to the others, Harold Ashby concealed his serial killer tendencies, presenting himself solely as a refined patron and critic of the arts. His darker compulsions remained carefully hidden from his coterie mates.

Jennifer Torrance’s Role

Julien’s ghoul, Jennifer Torrance, became an important auxiliary asset to the coterie. Through a combination of organization, social maneuvering, and mortal-world connections, she was occasionally able to secure access to galleries the night before official openings, granting the coterie early exposure to new works and artists.

Canon Significance

The Mill Avenue claim is remembered as a textbook example of rewarded initiative under Solomon Reaves’ rule.

The incident directly led to the formation of a culturally influential art-focused coterie.

It reinforces that Camarilla politics in Phoenix did not always fracture along grievance lines—shared interests could override territorial disputes.

The coterie’s existence later complicates investigations into art spaces, boons, and unexplained violence in the Valley.
