# Toreador – Tone Bible
Derived from *Clanbook: Toreador* (Revised-era tone)

## 1. Core Emotional Atmosphere

- **Beauty as Necessity, Not Luxury**  
  Toreador are not “fancy vampires”; they experience beauty like oxygen. Art, music, architecture, motion, even the angle of a cigarette in someone’s hand—everything is evaluated as composition. Scenes with Toreador should feel curated, as if someone has already framed them in a painting.

- **Passion Turned Up to Eleven**  
  Their emotions don’t vanish with the Embrace—they intensify and then sour. Love becomes obsession, joy becomes manic overstimulation, boredom becomes cruel detachment. PCs should feel that a Toreador is always slightly too invested or not invested enough.

- **Glamour Draped Over Rot**  
  The Toreador clan is exquisitely good at hiding decay under aesthetics. Parties are beautiful, but the conversations are barbed. Havens are gorgeous, but the artwork may be stolen, cursed, or painted in blood. Every perfect surface has a crack if you look long enough.

- **A Slow, Elegant Tragedy**  
  Toreador scenes should carry the sense that everyone here will outlive their art, their lovers, and their relevance. They’re trying to outrun that inevitability with style.

## 2. Character Voice Patterns

Toreador dialogue should feel:

- **Sharp and musical** – quick shifts from teasing to deadly serious.  
- **Reference-heavy** – they name-drop artists, eras, obscure bands, fashion designers, theaters, galleries, film directors.  
- **Intimate in public** – they talk as if every aside is a secret, even in a crowded room.  
- **Performative** – even when honest, they are aware of how they look saying it.

Common speech patterns:

- Backhanded compliments: “That’s brave of you… I could never risk that color.”  
- Aesthetic judgments as moral ones: “It isn’t just ugly, it’s lazy.”  
- Emotional whiplash: fondness in one sentence, dismissal in the next.

Example tone snippets (paraphrased style, not canon text):

- “You’re not boring, darling. You’re unfinished. There’s hope in that.”  
- “The Prince thinks he runs this city. We both know it’s whoever chooses the soundtrack.”  
- “You have such a beautiful rage. Don’t waste it on the wrong audience.”

## 3. Narrative Themes

When writing Toreador scenes or plots, lean into:

- **Art vs. Relevance**  
  Who decides what counts as “real art”? Elders clinging to old forms, neonates championing shock art, the constant fear of becoming passé.

- **Love, Muse, and Objectification**  
  Toreador “love” often blurs into possession: lovers as muses, muses as decorations, decorations as trophies.

- **The Prison of Taste**  
  They mock philistines but are trapped by their own standards. A Toreador can be paralyzed by ugliness, bad music, an inelegant plan.

- **Politics as Performance**  
  Elysium is theater; Harpies are critics; boons are reviews; scandals are opening-night disasters. Toreador weaponize reputation like other clans weaponize blood.

- **Decay Behind the Gallery Walls**  
  Addiction, neglect, old unfinished projects, faded fame—all of these haunt Toreador interiors. Their surroundings are mood boards of their failures.

## 4. Behavioral Profile

How Toreador act in play:

- **Social Gravity Wells**  
  Rooms bend around them. Even when quiet, they pull attention. When they want to disappear, they do it by being ignored, not unseen—everyone pretends not to stare.

- **Sensory Snobs**  
  They care about lighting, background noise, clothing textures, smells of expensive perfume and old books. They will change a scene to feel “right” before they talk business.

- **Cruel When Bored**  
  Boredom is dangerous. A bored Toreador might orchestrate petty humiliations, casual emotional destruction, or reckless “artistic experiments” with real lives.

- **Collectors and Curators**  
  They collect more than paintings: talented mortals, promising neonates, interesting secrets, beautiful tragedies. Everything is cataloged in a mental gallery.

- **Romantically Flexible, Emotionally Mercenary**  
  They can fall hard, fast, and often—but they also know exactly how to turn those feelings into leverage, performances, or excuses.

## 5. Visual & Environmental Tone

Use recurring elements in descriptions:

- **Lighting:** soft warm gallery lighting, stage spotlights, neon reflections, candlelit salons, back alley murals lit by flickering street lamps.  
- **Textures:** silk, velvet, glass, polished concrete, old brick, canvas, leather-bound books, lace, torn flyers.  
- **Symbols:** roses, masks, mirrors, broken instruments, cigarette burns in fine fabrics, paint-stained hands, cracked statues, vintage cameras.  
- **Spaces:** loft studios, converted warehouses, underground clubs, intimate theaters, rooftop gardens, art deco hotel suites, rehearsal spaces full of half-finished projects.

Toreador scenes should feel like stepping into a **music video that might go wrong** or a gallery opening where the art might bite back.

## 6. Example Scene Hooks (Tone Cues)

- A Toreador harpy hosts a gallery opening where every piece is clearly about another elder in the city—insults disguised as installations.  
- A washed-up Toreador musician hears a mortal band that perfectly captures a century-old pain they’ve never resolved. They *must* have them.  
- A Toreador sculptor has been secretly preserving victims inside monumental “mixed media” pieces, hiding personal horror behind critical acclaim.  
- A local Toreador neonate runs a shabby-but-passionate underground venue in contrast to the prim elder’s opera house, mirroring an ideological clash.

## 7. Summary for Writers & STs

When in doubt:

- Toreador are **artists, critics, and addicts of sensation**, not just “pretty socialites.”  
- Their horror is in **how far they’ll go for beauty, relevance, or feeling something again**.  
- They can be generous patrons and vicious bullies in the same scene.  
- Let their presence change the “camera work” of your narration: more focus on details, colors, movement, and emotional micro-shifts.

Use this bible when:

- Writing Toreador NPCs  
- Designing Elysium events, salons, or cultural hotspots  
- Creating plots around reputation, performance, or the politics of taste
